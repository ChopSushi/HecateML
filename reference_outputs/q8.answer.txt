## Facts
- Most public-key encryption algorithms used in banking rely on mathematical problems—integer factorization, discrete logarithms, and elliptic-curve discrete logarithms—that a sufficiently powerful quantum computer could solve using Shor's algorithm. [1] [VERIFIED]
- Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC) refers to cryptographic algorithms designed to be secure against quantum-based attacks; these are considered "quantum-resistant," though their security has not been mathematically proven. [1] [VERIFIED]
- Research in quantum computing—leveraging superposition and entanglement—has made significant progress, driving exploration into quantum hardware and the need for quantum-secure communications. [2] [VERIFIED]
- "Harvest Now, Decrypt Later" is a security community speculation where adversaries intercept and store encrypted data for future decryption once quantum hardware is capable. [UNVERIFIED]
- There is active debate regarding the timeline for "Q-Day"—the moment quantum computers break modern encryption—and whether financial institutions will transition to PQC standards before this deadline. [UNVERIFIED]
- Future security may shift toward "quantum key establishment," although the scalability and integration of such systems remain speculative. [3] [VERIFIED]

## Answer
The banking industry faces a significant, long-term risk to its core security infrastructure due to the potential of quantum computing. Because current digital banking relies on public-key encryption algorithms (such as RSA or Elliptic Curve Cryptography), the eventual development of a fault-tolerant quantum computer running Shor's algorithm could render these protections obsolete [1].

In response, the industry is exploring Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC), which focuses on developing algorithms resistant to quantum cryptanalysis [1]. While these algorithms are being developed, they are currently characterized as quantum-resistant rather than mathematically proven [1]. Additionally, researchers are investigating quantum key establishment as a potential evolution of traditional security protocols [3]. 

It is important to note that the concepts of "Harvest Now, Decrypt Later" tactics, the exact timeline for "Q-Day," and the operational challenges of integrating quantum-secure systems into legacy banking infrastructure are subject to industry speculation and ongoing debate, rather than established fact [2].
